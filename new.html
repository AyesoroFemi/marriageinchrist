KEEP MOVING FORWARD

"The LORD our God spoke to us in Horeb, saying: ‘You have dwelt long enough at this mountain"- Deuteronomy 1:6
Whenever the month of March is approaching, the image that comes to my mind is that of its literal meaning in its present continuous adjective - marching.
As we know, marching is a form of exercise that we engage in order to keep our muscles strong and toned to keep fit or attain fitness.
It is also done by the military as they advance towards a destination in a formation during the parade. Whichever way we look at it, it refers to ‘not standing still’, but ‘moving, advancing’ toward an objective.
From our earlier passage, we see that God does not want us to stay in the same spot. He wants us to keep moving in our relationships, especially our marital relationship.
After we have been married for a while, there is a tendency to keep things still, as we get inundated with responsibilities of the children, home, extended families, and in-laws while attempting to advance our careers at the same time.
Balancing work and family life tends to override everything else and keep things stagnant in the relationship. Marching is an action word; it takes intentionality to march forward against all the obstacles and pressures of life to keep the relationship alive - moving forward.
What did God instruct the children of Israel to do when confronted with the red sea, which was one of their obstacles from reaching their promised land? “And the LORD said unto Moses, wherefore criest thou unto me? Speak unto the children of Israel, that they go forward:” Ex. 14:15.
We must be determined to move forward! What are some of the obstacles that can be in our way of marching forward in our marriage?

1. Poor communication:
Communication is like the blood circulation in the body, and when blood circulation stops, the body parts start to die gradually. This is similar to when couples do not communicate effectively in their relationships. Today's busy schedule could be a big factor, especially if both partners are operating different work schedules, the tendency to miss out on communication may be very high. Thus, there is a need for intentionality, which might look like creating time alone together a couple of times a week to talk about current issues.
It will be good to have things jotted down so that the most pending issues can be talked over during this time. To create the time and space for these conversations, couples with young children can have the oldest one (if mature enough) watch the younger ones in another room or have these conversations when all of them have gone to school. Also, be intentional to communicate from the heart by calling and checking on each other daily during work time breaks. We may not even have anything to talk about in mind. It could be, just calling to check on you, just calling to say, "I love you."

2. Lack of intimacy:
Sometimes couples don't recognize the need to build space for intimate moments in their lives. Sex has long been recognized as a natural tranquilizer that also strengthens bonds between couples. Most of the time, couples expect that physical intimacy will be spontaneous. As we grow older, the spontaneity wanes.
The circumstances have to be cultivated to improve our mood; we have to remove all obstacles occasioned by the stresses of life.
Couples are sometimes advised to schedule time for emphasis. It should not be mechanical (just to fulfill all righteousness), but we need to communicate our preferences as we age. There is so much legitimate help and resources out there to improve our skill set in this area. When we truly enjoy something, we want more of it.
Physical intimacy is not only about sex alone, it’s also about touch and closeness between bodies. Romantic relationships can include a warm hug when parting or arriving at home, sitting close to each other to watch TV, holding hands to pray, cuddling/ sleeping close to each other( spooning position recommended). All these may require intentionality to engage daily and on a routine basis.

3. Growing apart:
As children of God, we are expected to keep growing in our Christian walk. Oftentimes, when couples fail to grow together in their relationship, they start to grow apart. It's important that we both encourage each other to grow together by attending church together, having daily devotion, and getting to be involved in the work of the ministry together.
All of these will keep both parties growing together and moving forward in the marriage. Outside influences: Issues with in-laws, friends, and family should be kept in the right places. Whenever problems arise, they should be resolved with input from both parties with agreement on how avoid/minimize extraneous influences in the relationship as they can be hindrances from keeping the marriage moving forward.

4. No demonstration of love and respect:
The Bible instructs husbands to love their wives like Christ loves the Church and gave himself up for her.
That's the example the Bible gave for how a husband should love his wife. In the same manner, the Bible encourages the wife to respect her husband.
The example given in the Bible of a respectful woman is that of Sara, and the way she demonstrated that respect from the Bible is that she referred to him in reference and respect by calling him Lord ;- 1Peter 3:6. I know for some it's easier to refer to our pastors that might even be younger than our husbands as "sirs", while we just call our husbands by their given names. Finding pet names that depict love and respect will be a tool in maintaining and keeping our marriage moving forward.
Engaging in all these will keep us empowered to overcome hindrances in our relationships, keep our marriage alive, and help us in marching forward for God's glory to be seen as God designed it to be.

Shalom!
MICM
